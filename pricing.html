Pricing Strategy
Alex Lamb for Videfy · April 2026
Pricing direction memo
Honeypot + three tiers + enterprise.
Included usage. Credit top-ups.
Launch with a credits-only Honeypot at the front, three subscription tiers (Creator · Pro · Team) with included monthly usage, then meter heavier actions with credit top-ups. Honeypot is the only credits-led surface — paid tiers stay clean. Built to serve four GTM avenues: Claude MCP, Honeypot, Avatar, and the Adobe Premiere Extension.
Monetization architecture

Three layers.
One coherent funnel.

The pricing model lives in three layers. Each layer has a job. Mixing them is what kills SaaS pricing pages.

①
Layer 1 — Acquisition
Honeypot
Low-friction entry. Credit-based. Designed for curiosity, first use, first value. Not the primary monetization model. Replaces the traditional free tier without parking users on zero revenue.
  • Pay-per-credit + promo packs
  • Generation only, no project persistence
  • Designed to convert into Layer 2
②
Layer 2 — Core recurring
Creator · Pro · Team · Enterprise
Subscription plans. Where recurring workflow value lives. Persistent projects, organization, repeatability, collaboration, and production-system value.
  • Predictable monthly subscription + included usage
  • Hybrid (sub + credits), not seat-only, not credits-only
  • 14-day Pro trial, no CC, no traditional free tier
③
Layer 3 — Expansion
Top-ups + metered heavy actions
Where margin grows. Top-ups for above-allowance use, metered heavy actions, future premium / advanced generation. Belongs to subscribed users, not just one-off honeypot users.
  • Per-action top-ups for above-allowance use
  • Credit packs (1k / 5k / 25k)
  • Future premium actions (advanced gen, handoff packaging)
The story in one line
Honeypot gets users in. Subscriptions hold recurring workflow value. Top-ups monetize spikes and expensive usage.
Free value layer — delivered through content, not product
Traditional freemium would put free features inside the product. We deliver free value through content instead: tutorials, tips, tricks, workflow breakdowns, and resources across Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, X, and YouTube — with Discord as the most active surface for frameworks, prompts, early access, and community feedback. The product has no permanent free tier. The content does.
The recommendation

Hybrid wins.
And it is not close.

Three pricing patterns are live in the market right now: subscription + included usage (Runway, Descript, CapCut Pro), per-seat business pricing (Adobe, Canva, Descript Business), and one-time license (DaVinci Resolve). For a workflow product with variable-cost AI actions, the strongest pattern is hybrid — predictable subscription plus metered usage for heavy actions.

✓
Why hybrid wins
Recommended
  • Easier to understand than credits-only
  • Better cost control than subscription-only
  • Familiar — Descript, Runway, CapCut already train this pattern
  • Supports both creator and team buyers
  • Keeps the homepage clean (no credit jargon)
✕
Why not credits-only
Rejected
  • Too abstract for first-time buyers
  • Feels risky and hard to budget
  • Hard to communicate value on the homepage in one line
  • Better as an internal meter than a top-level commercial story

One enterprise line.

Start instantly — Honeypot (credits-only front door)
What it is: low-friction entry. Pay-per-credit. Promo packs. Designed for curiosity, first use, first value — not primary monetization. Replaces the traditional free tier without parking users on zero revenue.

What it can do: generation only (image, video, avatar, voiceover via credits).
What it can't do (cannibalization guardrails): no persistent projects · limited project history · no team / collaboration · no approvals · no Adobe Premiere extension · no handoff / export packaging beyond basic · less favorable economics for repeat use.

Upgrade triggers (Honeypot → paid plan): user needs project persistence · weekly / repeat usage · storage or handoff structure · team workflow · top-ups become less economical than subscribing.

The three gating fences that matter most
1. Project persistence — separates Honeypot from Creator. "Save your work" is the first upgrade trigger.
2. Adobe Premiere Extension — separates Creator from Pro. This is the pro / agency unlock with no competitor parity.
3. Shared usage pool + approvals — separates Pro from Team. This is where seat-based pricing earns its price.

Honeypot vs. 14-day Pro trial

Two no-cost doors.
Different jobs.

Both entry points feel free. They are not the same. Honeypot proves the AI. Trial proves the workflow. Keep them distinct so positioning stays clean and the funnel stays readable.

How they feed each other
Cold → Honeypot → Trial → Paid. Homepage visitor tries Honeypot, burns through a credit pack, creates a project they want to keep. Upgrade prompt offers: "Start your 14-day Pro trial." Trial converts the now-qualified lead into a subscription. Honeypot spend is not wasted margin — it is attribution for a paid user with proven intent.
The risk if we do not distinguish them
Two "free-feeling" doors can confuse positioning if marketed flat. Fix: Honeypot lives on the homepage hero / product pages (cold acquisition). Trial lives on the pricing page + Pro tier CTA (warm, comparison-stage). Never cross them on the same surface.
Guardrails

What not to do
this launch cycle.

Six things that kill the model
1. Do not use credits-only as the primary monetization model. Honeypot is a constrained credit-based entry point — not the main plan.
2. No traditional unlimited free tier in v1. Honeypot replaces "free" without parking users on zero revenue.
3. Do not let Honeypot cannibalize Creator — keep persistence, collaboration, handoff, and team features behind the subscription wall.
4. Do not launch seat-only — cuts off the creator buyer and slows content motion.
5. Do not over-feature Enterprise on day one — use it as a sales-led capture line.
6. Do not make the paid pricing page explain credits — credits stay an internal meter at paid tiers.
7. Do not undercut Pro with too much in Creator — Creator should be enough to ship, not enough to never upgrade.
8. Do not match Adobe's price ladder — different positioning, different buyer, different story.

Open questions

Inputs needed
from product & finance.

1
Cost per heavy Claude action
What is the actual COGS of one feedback synthesis? Sets the credit price floor.
2
Cost per standard export (Adobe · CapCut · mobile · MP4)
Storage + processing per export. Sets the metered cost.
3
Storage model
Does Videfy host video files, reference cloud storage, or both? Storage cost shapes the Team tier especially.
4
Seat economics for Team
Base + seats vs. pure per-seat. Needs a small-team pricing test.
5
Annual cash-flow target
What % of revenue from annual vs. monthly in year 1? Sets annual discount aggressiveness.
6
Trial conversion target
What rate makes the trial-only-no-free-tier model work? Below threshold may force a free tier later.
7
Credit expiry policy
Credits never expire mid-cycle, but do unused credits roll into the next? Friendlier = roll. Cheaper = expire.
8
Top-up pricing
Fixed packs (1k / 5k / 25k credits) vs. metered overage at month end.

Honeypot-specific
open questions.

1
First-purchase credit pack
What is the price + size of the first credit pack a Honeypot user buys? Sets the on-ramp.
2
Which actions consume Honeypot credits
Generation only? Or also avatar / voiceover / upscale? Defines the funnel surface.
3
What's intentionally excluded
Project persistence, collaboration, team features, full handoff — confirm cannibalization guardrails.
4
Upgrade trigger
What event fires the upgrade prompt? "Save project," "return tomorrow," "share with team," top-up cost crosses subscription value?
5
Honeypot credit expiry
Do Honeypot credits expire faster than paid-tier credits? (Pressure to convert vs. user trust.)
6
Honeypot → plan credit conversion
When a Honeypot user upgrades, do unused credits convert into plan credits? Or zeroed?
7
Margin floor on one-off usage
What gross margin is required on a single Honeypot credit transaction to make the funnel sustainable?
